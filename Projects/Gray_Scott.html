The Gray-Scott model of a reaction-diffusion system

Complex dynamics can occur through relatively simple rules, Conway's Game of Life is a great example of this. On this page the Gray-Scott model is investigated, here the following chemical reactions are modeled:

\[ U + 2V \rightarrow 3V \] \[ V \rightarrow P \]

The concentrations of species U and V are followed and visualized. To do this a 2D grid is created where every point on the grid has a concentration of U and of V. The species then spread throughout the grid by diffusion. The diffusivity of U is usually twice the diffusivity of V. Reaction only occurs between species on the same grid point. The partial differential equations shown below are used to describe the concentrations of U and V over time.

\[ \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} = D_u \nabla^2 U - UV^2 + F(1-U) \] \[ \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = D_v \nabla^2 V - UV^2 - (F+k)V \]

In the equations \(D_u\) and \(D_v\) are the diffusivity of U and V respectively. The concentrations of the species are indicated by U and V itself. And finally F and k are the feed rate and kill rate respectively. The feed rate F determines how quickly U gets replenished and how quickly V gets depleted. While the kill rate k determines the speed at which V reacts away to form P.

The equations of the Gray-Scott model shown above are solved using the explicit Euler method. In the canvas below you can interact with the Gray-Scott model. On the left you can change the feed rate F and the kill rate k. The resulting concentrations of U are shown in blue and the resulting concentrations of V are shown in red on the canvas. You can inject species V by clicking on the canvas with the mouse. Several interesting combinations of F and k are:

  • Spots and loops: F = 0.018, k = 0.051
  • Moving spots: F = 0.014, k = 0.054
  • Chaos and holes: F = 0.034, k = 0.056
  • Mazes: F = 0.029, k = 0.057
  • Mitosis: F = 0.036, k = 0.060
